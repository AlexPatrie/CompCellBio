Computational Cell Biology

The Computational Cell Biology (CCB) resource formally brings together two foundational software packages, COPASI and Virtual Cell (VCell). The CCB platform will maintain and enhance these two widely used shared modeling resources with the research community. As the VCell and COPASI platforms were developed over the last two decades, researchers have come to recognize and appreciate the ability to explore quantitative experimental data within the context of specific mechanistic hypotheses and to use the predictive power of simulation for experimental design. Today computational analysis is a driving force in complex experiments while computational models are leading to the development of experimental methods.

Over the years, the VCell and COPASI development teams have been committed to educating our user communities on the value of mathematical modeling and how utilizing modeling techniques in their research. In parallel, the team has been actively broadening the scope its user community to a wide variety of cell biologists, thereby expanding the field of computational cell biology. In addition to bringing the teams of VCell and COPASI together to ensure their continued success, the CCB resource is committed to and focused on the dissemination, training and outreach of mathematical modeling resources for the growing cell and systems biology research communities. The resource will lend itself to promote sharing, collaboration, and support for standards in computational modeling.


COPASI software is for simulation and analysis of biochemical networks and their dynamics.


SpringSaLaD is for explicitly modeling binding events and state changes among multivalent molecules using Langevin Dynamics.


VCell is a comprehensive platform for modeling cell biological systems using nonspatial (ODE or stochastic ) or spatial (PDE or stochastic) methods.

02-18-22. Congratulations to Boris Slepchenko on his latest publication with CCAM alumnus Masoud Nickaeen, A model of actin-driven endocytosis explains differences of endocytic motility in budding and fission yeast. In this article they showed that a molecularly explicit model of forces exerted by actin filaments on endocytic invaginations explains the differences in endocytic invaginations between budding and fission yeast.

12-03-21 Ann Cowan and Les Loew are authors on another paper with the Carlier lab. This paper was published in The Biophysicist, Teaching Mathematical Modeling of Cellular Systems with the VCell MathModel. Congratulations on this paper and their continued collaborative work.

09-09-2021 Les Loew is co-author on a paper with collaborators from the Pollard lab at Yale, just published online in Biophysical Journal (PMID 34509503). The work was started by Les some time ago to computationally explore molecular details of actin assembly and was recently revived by a graduate student in the Pollard lab.
